    I have tried twice in my lifetime. Here is what I would and may still do today.
    The main focal points are:
    1. Keep costs to a minimum.
    2. This will attract many more students (highly marketable) to GA.

    Today’s (2012) Guidelines:
    1. Get ground school taken and passed but must include online video training (self-paced) and simulator practice.
    2. Focus on Sport Pilot’s License (minimizes cost and maximizes full pilot’s license training future).

  • Jeff S.

    I really wish there had been a good guide somewhere about what makes a good instructor.  I’ve seen a lot of “got to get along/be a good fit” but that’s not even close to being it.  With my first instructor, I had a lot of fun and got along with great, but found out she wasn’t really interesting in figuring out what I was doing wrong.

    1) Find an instructor who is a TEACHER first, and a PILOT second.
    2) Never accept “I’ll get it if I just keep trying.”  If you’re having a problem, ask the instructor what you’re doing wrong and if they don’t know or just say “practice” then go elsewhere.  Immediately.  You can and should practice doing it right, but if you don’t know what you’re doing wrong, there’s nothing to practice.
    3) From the start, I’d have gone to a different airport that didn’t have constant 10 knot winds (good practice but not good when you’re trying to LEARN to land consistently), a no T&G on weekends rule, and haze/fog nearly every morning and late afternoon.  Four landings in an hour and a half of pattern work is just a waste of money.
    4) I’d have spoken to SEVERAL students at all phases of their instruction BEFORE hiring an instructor.  I wouldn’t have my 3rd instructor be someone who got his CFI literally 2 weeks before I started and who had NO criteria for what was “good enough to solo.”
    5) I’d have figured out that the “averages” aren’t actually average because 80% of the people drop out – and those are likely the “higher time to ticket” people.  If a majority of those who started actually finished, the TRUE average would be more like 80-100 hours, not the 50-60 that’s often quoted.
    6) I’d have tried to get a better idea of what “ground” time is.  It isn’t just time spent learning on the ground; it is apparently the time the instructor takes to walk out to the plane, get the seats adjusted, walk back, enter your time into the computer to print your bill, etc.  I spent nearly 30-40% of the Hobbs time as “ground” and that wasn’t including any actual, sit in a classroom, ground instruction.  Sort of like baggage fees, it is just a way to increase the bill.
    7) I’d have been a bit quicker on the trigger with my last instructor, instead of finding out that he didn’t like my rudder usage 30 hours in.  He thought I just wasn’t good rather than having been TAUGHT incorrectly.
    8) I’d have found a good TEACHER (which my last instructor was) but not necessarily one who thought that I needed to meet commercial PTS standards before soloing.

    As it is, I flew just over 80 hours with 4 primary instructors, 1 on my demo flight, and one filling in one lesson – and never did get to solo.  I finally figured out what the problem was (primarily looking at the wrong thing) in my last two lessons when I finally INSISTED that we do something other than go around the pattern, which I did for about 65 hours.

    With probably about 25-30 hours left and probably about to solo, I had to quit because I’d spent $17,000 and just couldn’t afford it any more.

    Now, if I go back, I’ll have a bunch of “de-rusting” to do before those 30 or so hours of requirements – meaning that I’ve probably got another $8,000 and 40 hours.  (and yeah, we’re talking old 172’s here – not glass)
