Modern trainers (i.e. 172’s) are very spin-forgiving, and will usually self-recover if you just go hands-off as soon as you feel the plane get away from you. Even if you get into a full spin you can often break it by just giving the yoke a solid push all the way in. 

It becomes a much more dicey situation in other types of aircraft, and knowing proper recovery procedure is key, but the 172 will do everything it can to keep you from killing yourself.

Here are the recovery items in expanded form:

Rudder – opposite direction of spin
Control Wheel – ailerons neutral, yoke full forward
Throttle – idle

*After recovery*

Rudder – release
Control Wheel – as required
Throttle – as required

]]> By: Mark C http://www.m0a.com/proper-spin-recovery/comment-page-1/#comment-5581 Wed, 08 Jun 2011 18:11:00 +0000 http://www.m0a.com/?p=3422#comment-5581 Jason, I was hoping for some spin video – maybe you should take the new cameras out and try some.

My first flight school required spin training early in the lessons. I literally worried myself sick that I’d screw up and kill us, but I didn’t and after having done spins I gained a great deal of confidence in my ability and the aircraft, and I found I actually kind of enjoyed them. What I was taught, is that when you enter a spin, the first thing you should do is to stop doing whatever you’re doing to the airplane – neutralize the controls, and take a deep breath. In many aircraft this alone will break the spin, especially if it’s not fully developed or you’ve only gone through a couple rotations. The Champ I trained in would recover all by itself just by doing that. If the aircraft continues to spin, then gently and smoothly apply opposite rudder until the spin breaks – slamming rudder in can actually cause the spin to reverse. Once the rotation has stopped, check your airspeed, smoothly bring power back in, and gently raise the nose to stop your descent. But most importantly, always have sufficient altitude before practicing spins. A simple 1.5 rotation spin and recovery will easily cause you to lose 1000 feet.

]]> By: CLD http://www.m0a.com/proper-spin-recovery/comment-page-1/#comment-5517 Wed, 01 Jun 2011 00:02:00 +0000 http://www.m0a.com/?p=3422#comment-5517 Various authors have indicated that the inclinometer is not a reliable indication of the direction of spin.  In other words, “stepping on the ball” will not always give you the right answer.  (Refer to Bill Kershner’s “The Student Pilot’s Flight Manual” or Rich Stowell’s writings concerning spins, for example.)  Kershner found that the position of the ball during a spin depends on which side of the instrument panel the slip skid indicator is located.  This makes sense if the centrifugal force caused by the spin is the dominant force acting on the ball.  Under these circumstances, spinning will cause the ball to be “thrown” away from the center of rotation — to the left if the indicator is on the left side of the panel and to the right if it is on the right side.  The direction of spin is unimportant.  (A merry-go-r0und provides a simple illustration.  It makes no difference if you spin clockwise or counterclockwise — you’ll always feel like you’re being slung away from the center.)
