Class D Airspace – Radio Communications

by Jason Schappert | 22 comments

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

Class D airspace is the smallest airspace where you can talk to a ground controller and tower. Many students and pilots alike coming from primarily uncontrolled environments have trouble when it comes to talking to someone else especially when you have to readback certain instructions. Fly along with me as we grab the ATIS and contact ground to get our taxi clearance.

  • http://twitter.com/t0r0nad0 PJ Gustafson

    John, that is correct, you are letting ATC know that you have the most up-to-date information. With an ATIS broadcast, it is not just the weather info that they are worried about, it’s also the NOTAMs they include at the end of the broadcast. If you are flying into an airport with an ASOS or AWOS instead of ATIS, you just say you ‘have the information’. If you do not give them an indication that you have the ATIS/ASOS/AWOS, the controller is required to give you the winds and altimeter.

  • http://twitter.com/t0r0nad0 PJ Gustafson

    Good video, Jason. I did all of my training at two Class D airports – KDWH (Hooks Airport, Tomball, TX) for the first 6 hours or so, and KSGR (Sugar Land, TX) for the remainder. In addition to that, when I would travel for business I would go to the local airport and take discovery flights, and all of those were at either Class D (KDVT, KSUS, KENW, KSDM) or Class C (KBIL, KGEG) airports. So I became very familiar with towered airport communications, and like Chris (are you In the Pattern’s Chris, BTW?), I enjoy talking on the radio almost as much as I enjoy flying.

    There are three things that REALLY helped me develop my radio skills:

    1) I got a scanner and listened. Like I said, I used to travel a lot for business, so before a trip I would go print out the airnav pages for the airports I was going to and the taxi diagrams. I would then listen while waiting for my flight and follow the aircraft progress on the diagram. I would also go visit the local GA airports, park at the end of the runway, and watch and listen as well. You get to learn a lot doing that, not just about towered and non-towered communications, but it also helps you learn to identify aircraft types as well.

    2) Liveatc.net – While I prefer to be at the airport in person and listening on the scanner, listening to airport traffic on liveatc.net can be educational as well when you can’t get to the airport itself. Later, when I started doing some training in Houston’s Class B, I would go back and find the liveatc.net recording of myself to critique it.

    3) VATSIM and FS9 – Using Microsoft Flight Sim and the VATSIM network (www.vatsim.net), I got to practice radio communications in a practical environment. VATSIM is free, it just requires FS9, FSX, or XPlane, a headset, and a broadband internet connection. People go online and act as air traffic controllers and you can go online as a pilot and actually talk back and forth with these people. It’s very professional and they use real-world ATC rules and procedures. It’s great practice… and it’s much easier to replace some bent electronics if you mess up here than it is to replace bent aluminum in the real world.

    To illustrate my point, I made my very first real-world radio call while on my 2nd or 3rd Discovery Flight, flying into KDVT on Christmas Eve several years ago, with all of 2 hours in my log book. After I made the call, the instructor told me that she had 100-hr private pilots who couldn’t do it as well as I had. It works, and it’s cheap!

  • http://twitter.com/t0r0nad0 PJ Gustafson

    Well, telling Ground does a few things:

    1) A lot of times at Class D airports that are not busy, Ground and Tower are the same person, so in essence you ARE telling the tower.

    2) Whether it’s the same person or not, they are sitting right next to each other, so ground can relay the message to tower. This helps the tower to know what to expect in advance so that they can make your departure easier.

    3) At airports with multiple active runways, telling the ground controller which way you’re going lets them know to sequence you to the runway that gives you the most expeditious departure. For example, at KDWH, there are parallel runways, 17L/35R and 17R/35L. Nearly all of the parking is on the east side of the field, so by telling them you’re a west departure, they will guide you to 17R/35L so you can make a quick turn and go.

    It’s also for this reason that you should tell tower where you are parking before you land. That way they can let you land on the runway that will give you the shortest taxi.

    BTW – If you are a pilot of any level and have never visited a tower or other ATC facility, do so ASAP. In my experience, they are always very welcoming and you can learn a lot, especially by visiting the tower at the airport you fly out of regularly. It takes a little more advance notice and planning to visit a TRACON facility or Class B tower, but I’ve been able to visit a couple of Class D towers recently just by calling them up, telling them that I’m a pilot in the area, and asking if I can come up. It’s great to see things on the other end of the microphone!
