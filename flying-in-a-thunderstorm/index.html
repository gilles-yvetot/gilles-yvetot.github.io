Flying In A Thunderstorm?!?

by Jason Schappert | 61 comments

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

Maybe I’m just losing my mind… Why would anyone even get close enough to a thunderstorm to have to worry about this? It’s an interesting question that was posed to me and I’d love to hear your opinion of it. Check out the video then PLEASE leave a comment below with what your stance is. – Jason

Text Transcript

Hey everybody. Jason Schappert of MzeroA.com, and I have a question that I wanna ask you guys. my viewers, listeners, watchers readers. As you guys know, I’m getting ready to release three brand new books. One of them is the predecessor to Pass Your Private Pilot Checkride, which is Pass Your Instrument Pilot Checkride and one of my editors sat down with me today and was talking through some stuff… what was good and what was a little bit off, he thought.

One of the questions that came up was, “Jason, you answer this particular question. What would you do if you flew into a thunderstorm completely different than in any other book we have in the aviation library?” Everybody in every other aviation book that he could find, the procedure was, if you fly into a thunderstorm… which sounds ridiculous enough already, we’re gonna get to that. But if you fly into a thunderstorm, you accelerate to your maximum turbulence penetration speed (a fancy way of saying VA), and continue on, not making any turns or anything whatsoever.
I don’t really like that answer very much, and I was bound to go a different route than the book partly because I don’t really believe in that. I think, first off, if you fly yourself into a thuderstorm, that had to lead up. There was a chain of events that lead to that. These thunderstorms — whether they are embedded thunderstorms or not — you can see these storm systems from way off. You’re an instrument rated pilot, and you plow through some of that stuff, you’re kind of asking for it as it is.

My point is, never even let it reach that point. The turbulence is going to get severe… downdrafts, updrafts can get so bad before you even get to the point where you’re actually in the thunderstorm and this is miles out, you can experience this stuff. You can make a shallow turn and turn back around to you know where the weather is good, which is right behind you where you came from. Don’t continue on, to the point where you’re inside of the thunderstorm. That’s absolutely ridiculous. You’re gonna know 20 miles out, 15 miles out, that you’re approaching the thunderstorm and you’re gonna feel the effects of it from that far out.

So, I really believe the idea of accelerating to your turbulence penetration speed or VA and just plow it on through it is absolutely ridiculous. I’m anxious what you guys think, whether you side with the books or with you side with myself… it doesn’t matter. I’d love to have a debate about it, that’s what I really want to spark. But I stand firm in my belief in that you’re gonna have plenty of warning signs before that it even happens and make a shallow bank. It could be 5 or 10 degrees of bank, but a very nice shallow bank to get you out of there and get you back around. There’s simply is no sense in flying into a thunderstorm.

So, beneath this video guys, is the comment box. Go ahead and, if you’re in MzeroA.com, I can guarantee a response from me. If you’re watching this video in Viddler or Youtube, I can’t promise a response, it’s hard to check so many websites but if you’re in MzeroA.com, right now watching this video, leave me a comment below and you know I’d get back to you. I’d love to hear your opinion about this guys. This kind of debate, this kind of discussion is gonna make us a safer pilot.

So, with that guys, remember, a good pilot is always learning. Have a great day guys. See ya.
