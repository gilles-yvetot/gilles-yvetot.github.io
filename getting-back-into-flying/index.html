In this weeks episode I interview Chris Findley of MyFlightCoach.com talking about his specility of “getting back into flying” You can tell instant why Chris is so successful as a flight coach specifically in helping pilots return to the skies.

In This Interview We Discuss:

Overcoming Flight Training Snags and Delays
Dealing With Financial Problems While Flight Training
How To Start Back Where You Left Off

Put that together with what I was seeing happening in a lot of other sectors, where there is life and career coaching,or financial coaching or business CEO-type of coaching and the rise of webinars and the rise of multimedia, I realized a lot of people in the aviation sector are not making use of these tools. So, MyFlightCoach.com is trying to tie together those things and answer those questions about getting back into flying among others and how did the process work. So it’s really more of a passion trying to help as much people as I can get back in the air.

A lot of these are people who haven’t flown in quite some time. They just take flight reviews just to keep their certificates and get medicals just for the same fact. They don’t ever do any flying. You got someone who hasn’t flown in twenty-four months wanting a flight review.

Chris:

Well, I think the best thing a CFI can do in that situation is to lay everything out in the table at that first meeting. Look at the logbook, review the history, talk to the person just a little bit. Most of the times, folks jump into the airplane just to get it done. We tend to maybe go faster through the process. What I do, is I sit down with them, go through the logbook and talk about their last experience flying if it’s been twenty months or twenty-two months and coming up with an end date for that VFR. We’ll talk about that a little bit. If I see that, I’m alwaysvery up front with them saying, “YOu know the FAA’s minimums of 1 hour ground and 1 hour flight is just that, it is a minimum. What we need to look at, is what’s gonna make you the safest and the best pilot.”

I’ve had folks say to me, “Well, I don’t wanna go through that process. I’m getting someone out to fly with me, whatsoever. He’ll sign me off after an hour.” That’s okay, maybe I’m not the best fit for them. But I think we need to be very up front and say, “Hey, look, if you haven’t flown in 24 months, that VFR will take you 3 hours of air.” Say that, and say, “Well, it depends… let’s fly for an hour and see where you are.” But we need to really… if the VFR is gonna mean anything, then we need to actually follow the guidelines that we’ve been given on that and not just sign them off. For me, an endorsement for me is, I’m putting my name on the line as a CFI. I am testifying that this person is qualified for whatever endorsement I’m giving, even if it’s a VFR.

So, I always say, if you’re not flying everyday and this is not a CFI or a commercial pilot or somebody, I usually state my personal minimum of 2 hours of flight and 2 hours of ground. I’ve kinda mapped out what I do with those 2 hours and we start there. I remind them that you’re not gonna fail the VFR, I’m not gonna report it to the FAA or anything like that. But this is about making you the best, most confident and safest pilot that we can have and I’m gonna partner with you in that. That about outlines my approach to flight instruction and what I do with MyFlightCoach.com. I use that word very deliberately. I want to be the coach here, and assist them in doing that. I think being very up front is the best way to go in that situation.

Jason:

It was just a matter of bringing them back up to the surface and help him get started, but he was literally…. an 8 or 9-hour flight review just before he got his confidence up. I was confident in him to a certain degree but it was all his head I was fighting. It was hard playing with those mental triggers of people like you said, who may feel like they failed and didn’t do it or they were discouraged, because the flight will take longer.

Chris:

My chief concern was, “Am I gonna be a threat to myself or others?” It’s like me on a golf course. You’d never wanna see that because it’s a threat to myself and others. I didn’t want that here and the flying. So it was very important and I think to drive that home is a VFR, for those that have really gone a long time between them so it’s very important. It may not be that you are not the right fit or I’m not the right fit for a particular person, and I think that’s okay.

This is going to be a longer response to this one because it’s kinda multi-faceted but it’s simply stated.

What do you do if you had a financial snag? Flying is expensive. It’s hard to budget for, people don’t always make the smallest financial decision. They wanna fly that beautiful glass panel when they start right off, when they should be flying the 150 or the Cherokee 140 so they are able to save some money first.

But what do you with a student who has hit a financial snag? What’s the best way to go about it?

Chris:

Well, you know, I was one of those students as well. That was always an issue right in there, never too far from the top of my mind. “How long is it gonna take? I’m not learning this as fast as I should and I should keep moving.” If you hit a financial snag, again it depends on what’s causing the snag. Has there been some situations that you lost income or some situations that you’re simply plateau’d out and you’re spending a lot of hours learning landings or whatever. I found that with the financial snag, what people do is… it’s sort of a psychological question. You’re dealing with numbers and hourly rates and things that are usually a lot higher than many people are used to dealing with, and it adds up faster than they realize and it catches them a little off-guard. You’d get that $350 bill for the two weeks’ period, say, and it’s, “Aw, that’s a lotta money.” SO it begins to kind of work on them.

I think the best thing to do… a good CFI will do this, I’m glad to do this with folks in MyFlightCoach.com.. is to sit down and do the very best as much as you can for what it’s gonna cost and don’t leave it at the big numbers. That’s what people a lot of times do. They leave it at, “Oh, it’s gonna cost seven grand or whatever, five grand depending on the plane, to get my license.” Don’t leave it there. Divide that up over a period of time and find out how much monthly we’re talking about because that’s where it’s gonna hit you.

And then I always encourage folks: “If it’s tight for you, be patient, wait. Save up some of that money so you take that pressure off yourself where you’re worried that, ‘Oh, gosh, I’m going to do another hour of touch and go’s.’ You want that there, so be patient, do some savings, set your priorities and begin to work that in financially so that you’re not completely stressed out trying to make it work financially.” So plan your finances.

Another thing that you can do to save money on you flight training is to come to your lessons prepared. So if you’re spending 30 minutes or half your lessons reviewing things that you could’ve done on the ground then you’re drawing money away. Be sure you’re prepared for that lesson. I’d even rather have a student say, “Hmm, can we go tomorrow because I’m not prepared?” I’d much rather have that experience than getting up and say, “Oh, here we go, recapping this again.” because we’re wasting time and we’re wasting money, and both of them are precious resources to us all.

One more thing I would say is practice on the ground. When I came back in for my flight review, one of the things that I had to get in my brain again were procedures. When I understood, my motor skills came back pretty well. I could fly the airplane but what I get flustered on, like some other folks I worked with getting back into flying, are the procedures. I can remember step one, step two but three, four and five are a little fuzzy… oh, I remember how that procedure ends, seven and eight. So I just use a regular old flight simulator at my desk, and have the checklist for the airplane with me and I would just run through the flow. By the time I got to the airplane the next time, I had it. You don’t have to spend a lot of time in front of the simulator, just flying the simulator but use it as a tool to get your procedures back. You’re more prepared that way.

You know, financial snags when they come, often we panic. I would say, sit down with your instructor and see what you can work out with that. Do you need to reduce the frequency of the flying? Do we need to honestly take a break and do some more saving? Every situation is different but I found out for myself, that it’s a matter of planning ahead and I found that it’s one of the best things to do. So get back into it, save some, do a little bit of savings, get a few hours ready to go and knock those out.

The cost of flying is always an issue for everyone, and I always want to say, “Yeah, but compared to what?” If we’re talking about comparing flying to tennis, it’s expensive. But, I always try and make them realize, “Look at the privilege and the joy that you’re gonna gain from flying. You’re flying an airplane, stop and think about that for a minute.” We’ve only been doing this for a little over a hundred years in the whole history of humanity and you get to be a part of doing that. Every time somebody passes me with a $20,000 Harley Davidson, I think, “You could’ve been a pilot and have $15,000 left over.”

Chris:

I think, with the written test, it’s all about repetition. The two approaches to a written that are helpful, kind of the long and the short approach. Most people go with some sort of prep where we’ll throw questions at them and they will learn the responses and hopefully also learn material along the way. The other thing as an instructor that I find crucial is that sometimes, a bit of an issue is that we cant stop there with the test prep. I found out that it’s the best, and continually work through that in your problem areas and develop a system.
