The Day Mogas Died

by Jason Schappert | 5 comments

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

There was one point in time where mogas (automotive fuel) was aviation’s saving grace. I remember when Avgas hit $7.35 here at my local airport, the flexibility to use our mogas STC saved the business.

Sadly after the implementation of ethanol into automotive gas I and many other well respected individuals in the aviation industry have deemed mogas “unsafe” for aviation purposes.

Ethanol is an alcohol derived from corn that reacts poorly when introduced to rubber.

An Example

I long time student of mine had been using mogas in his Cherokee 140. Throughout our flight training we never had a problem using mogas. After ethanol was added to every fuel station in the area we had no choice but to give it a try. It wasn’t long before we found out the side effects.

The ethanol had been slowly eating away at the rubber gasket on the right wing fuel sump. As it began to leak all of the fuel landed on the tire. Sitting this way for a few days when it came time to fly we found an empty right wing, leaking fuel sump, and a tire that had a hole eaten right through it!

Shortly after replacing the right fuel sump the left one went bad, followed by the right one again! (vicious cycle)

One Mishap

Although no accidents have been reported, a gentleman did have a mishap while flying his experimental aircraft with rubber fuel bladders. The ethanol ate right through the bladders over a period of time and eventually caused fuel exhaustion in flight.

Conclusion

I still know of a few barnstormers using mogas. Objectively speaking this is not an activity I promote. The days of mogas are over, keep it in the automobiles where it belongs.
