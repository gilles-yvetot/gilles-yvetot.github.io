Crosswind Landings

by Jason Schappert | 25 comments

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

Lets face it… Crosswind Landings are one of the most difficult landings to master. Why is that? You have to think… Your scenario is different everytime. Gusty winds, steady winds, right crosswinds, left crosswinds etc. A Crosswind Landing is very much a “touch” maneuver. Meaning you’ve got to know the right amount of pressure to apply on the rudder and aileron combination to keep that airplane flying straight down the runway.

Too much rudder and you end up side-loading the airplane. Not enough rudder and you find yourself dancing along the edge of the runway as you fight to get the plane under control.

In this video I’ll share with you some crab and side slip techniques to make your next crosswind landing your best!

    So in MI I rarely get the winds going right down the runway so >95% of my landings are crosswind landings. So if my crosswind landings are always high that means the students are the same. So as was wonderfully shown you must have a crab angle on downwind, base and final. Turning base to final I let mother nature push me onto my final approach leg. Wind from the left on a left pattern turn early and let the wind push me onto final. Wind from the right lag the turn and let the wind blow you to final. I do not use the crab method and then switch to the side slip. I love muscle memory. So when on final, as far out as I can I side slip all the way to flare and touchdown and keep the nose pointed down. Drifting left with a right crosswind more right aileron. I am using rudder to keep the airplane aligned with the extended centerline. This allows for me to make little adjustments. I also with gusty winds will use as little a flap setting as possible. As I slow down, with flaps the wind will affect me more with more drag. I have found with low time PP and students that the crap/sideslip transition is too many things happening when close to the runway. I have seen students put in the totally opposite aileron and rudder to what is needed and then the landing becomes a soft field landing off runway. Yuk. So that is my take.   • RVR5000

    It should be emphasized that the amount of rudder you apply is whatever it will take to align the longitudinal axis of the aircraft with the centerline of the runway AND enough bank to get the component of lift to balance the lift from the fuselage that happens when you have the rudder applied. As you are flaring and slowing down, you will need to increase the rudder pressure to keep the airplane’s longitudinal axis aligned with the runway and start DECREASING the bank as you get slower and slower until touchdown. The number 1 error I see with this maneuver is not decreasing the bank and the airplane starts to accelerate sideways into the wind and you touchdown skipping upwind. No books cover Crosswind Landings in this detail and it’s just a matter of balancing forces. If you look at any Slip, adding rudder creates an angle of attack with the fuselage which is a lifting force and the airplane will turn uncoordinated if you don’t balance that force out. You balance it by banking in the opposite direction and getting a component of lift (Lift Horizontal) to balance the lift from the fuselage. The fuselage is a lousy lifting device so it will not take a lot of bank to balance this force out. As you get slower and slower, you will notice the fuselage forces get smaller and smaller which means LESS bank angle. Apply this logic to a crosswind landing. Next time you are practicing crosswind landings on a real strong crosswind day, note which side of the centerline you touchdown on if it’s not perfect. MOST errors are upwind of the centerline because nowhere is it written what to do with the bank as you get slower and slower. I heard someone tell me once that you apply enough bank to balance the crosswind. Umm, no. You do NOT balance a Force with a Velocity. Horizontal Component of Lift does not balance a Crosswind. It’s one of those apples and oranges things. Lift Horizontal is a Force which is in pounds and a Crosswind is a speed which is in knots and one can not be applied in the same vector model.
