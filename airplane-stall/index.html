“Maybe we should wait and practice stalls on the next lesson!” Is something a student of mine recently said. We’ve developed this fear of stalling. But the reality of it is… Stalls really aren’t that scary and what you’re going to learn in the video below is we practice stalls to practice recoveries! Not to practice getting into a stall! Follow along with me in the video below as I show you a power on (departure stall) and a power off (landing stall).

  • Mark C

    Hi Jason,

    Good stuff, as always. I’m a low time student (22 hours), and when I started flight training, I was so afraid of stalls and spins that I got physically ill during my first 4 lessons. What helped me was first, successfully doing them, second, my instructor explaining that a power off stall is what we do every time we land an airplane, and third and maybe most, my instructor took me out and we did the “falling leaf” where you take the aircraft into a power off stall and then just hold the stick back, and tap the rudder pedals as necessary to keep the plane level. At first it scared the spit out of me, but after I did it for a while, I started to enjoy it. A few lessons later when we did spin training, I was nervous, but not frightened, and actually kind of enjoyed the experience, and pride and confidence that came from doing the drills and spins made me a much more relaxed and confident student when we got back to more practical lessons. Most people who are really afraid of stalls, especially power on, are doing them wrong, they’re hauling back on the yoke too abruptly. You should never move any control in an airplane too abruptly, everything should be smooth and even. If you yank the stick back, the stall is very dramatic and tries to become a spin almost immediately, and the nose drop will raise your blood pressure. Ease it back just a little bit slower, and it’s a lot more comfortable. And, if you’ve done spin training and you do get a little wild, it’s not so scary because you know you can recover.

  • Thomas

    First off, get plenty of altitude before doing this… 1,500 feet minimum is what I remember reading, but I like to go up to 3,000. Altitude is my friend.

    Clearing turns are important. I turn to the left first watching for traffic and then back around to the right all the while scanning for traffic. The next thing I’m thinking of is to make sure to stay coordinated in flight which means to use the rudder and **center** that ball. By ** not** doing that you can get into a spin … not good. And flying coordinated is always a good thing.

    The recovery is key and is done by gently releasing back pressure. Gently is the operative word as you want to try and maintain as smooth a recovery as possible… remember, most stalls are happen near the ground, and you don’t want to hit the ground while recovering. And a plane can stall at any speed when the cord of the wing exceeds the maximum angle of attack relative to the wind.

    Also, for both type of stalls, I add power, take the carb heat out and then go for any flaps… allowing the speed build up and slowly removing flaps after airspeed builds up.

    Lastly, I pick a heading and try to maintain that heading throughout the maneuver.

    Great video … I am always learning from these.

    Thanks,

    Thomas

But the number one thing that alleviated my fear was not doing the stalls themselves, but in flying paper airplanes and rubber powered model airplanes. Tune into the airplane, and it will not surprise you. After all, it wants to fly.
