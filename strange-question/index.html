Strange Question: What Would You Do In This Situation

by Jason Schappert | 13 comments

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

Ok so i’ll let the video do most of the explaining here. Take a look at it and let me know what you would have done in the comment box below.

You’re going to like this one it really makes you think!

If you’d like to learn more about the powers of wake turbulence check this out

Text Transcript

Hey there everybody, Jason Schappert of MzeroA.com. I wanted to talk to you, actually I wanted to ask you guys a question, about what you would do in this situation? Kinda like last week, the blown tire. I had another.. not really a mishap, but something came up that normally doesn’t come up when you’re learning to fly. At least in my 3000, 4000 hours of flying I never experienced anything like this yet. So I was flying with a student… wait, let me backtrack a bit. We’re all familiar with wake turbulence. Just like a boat produces wake and those wakes come up, airplanes gonna produce a wake as well.

That’s something we commonly try to avoid by taking off above the wake and climb up above it. On landing, we try to land beyond their points, stay above the glide path, etc. You can learn more about that in other videos I’ve done.

What happened in this situation was, we were on a right downwind for runway 18. While we’re doing that, a C-130 which was just in a quick touch and go on runway 36… the winds were calm, the tower cleared him to do it. So he’s doing a touch and go on runway 36. Here we are, we’re on a downwind basically parallel with him, preparing to land on 18. The tower said, “Caution. Wake turbulence.” I mean, the C-130 is a big old airplane. So, I got to thinking, I never go to learn about approaching wake turbulence head on. The C-130 just took off this way and we’re trying to land that sameÂ  direction. Normally you both land on the same direction or taking off in the same direction, not opposing like that.

What we ended up doing is we just asked the tower, “Can we just do a 360?” I was just confused and my student was like, “We’ve never read about that kind of situation in your books or anything like that.” So maybe you can be the first to talk on that subject. What would you do in that situation? You know, a C-130 has just taken off at 36, and you’re trying to land at 18. So how can you avoid that wake turbulence? What we did was we just did a 360 on downwind, we got cleared by tower to do that. It gave us about extra 3 minutes. Plenty of time, and landed without a snag. But what would you do in that situation?

Leave me a comment below. You got a big airplane producing a lot of wake taking off 36 and you’re landing on 18, opposite of what we learned in the books. Leave me a comment below and tell me what you think. Always remember, a good pilot is always learning. See you guys later.

  • Koehn

    Since wake turbulence descends at roughly five hundred feet per minute, waiting someplace is the thing to do. You don't want to be doing your 360 in a place where the wake turbulence might be blown by the prevailing wind, so if there's a right crosswind do your 360 on the right side of the field. That way the turbulence will be blown to the left and away from your aircraft.

  • http://twitter.com/xenonax Troy Knight

    Waiting certainly was the prudent thing to do. Thinking this over, with calm winds I would expect the wake to descend and not really drift anywhere else. The C-130 has a 1830 fpm ascent (according to Wikipedia) and as such even if you had planned to land beyond the point their wheels left the ground you would likely be flying through the wake to get there. Another option could have been to request 36, which still gives a delay for the wake to dissipate.

    Landing behind a heavy aircraft 2 mins

    Wake vorticites tend to descend at a rate of 300 to 500fpm for the first 30 seconds then spread out and downward at a much slower rate as they begin to dissipate.

    So 3 and 2 mins are pretty safe
