  • mzeroa

    Close… Try this instead:

    1. Clearing turns
    2. Set RPM to whatever get’s you to Va in level cruise (will vary based on weight) (also I don’t add power going into the turn. I understand the reasons why however in the 150 it’s not needed maybe in your plane it will be
    3. Begin roll to the left first
    4. work in 3 full rolls of trim (from top to bottom = 1 roll)
    5. Place nose beautifully on the horizon.
    6. Fly with your fingertips manage and monitor
    7. Anticipate your heading by 10 degrees or so
    8. Work the 3 rolls of trim out

    Jason

    OK, the bottom line is…your method is cheating your students by not teaching the basic airmanship skills of adding back-pressure in a Steep-Turn and then releasing that back-pressure when rolling out. The simple reason not to teach this is because some student (maybe only one out of 10) may revert back to that teaching when flying in the pattern or just sight-seeing at low altitude with a few friends in the airplane….then to make things easier on himself, he just adds a few rolls of the trim wheel and doesn’t fly the aircraft like you and I were taught. The next thing you know, he does a low altitude accelerated stall which leads to a spin into the ground. Flying with your “finger tips” in this situation would not give you that extra feedback that you need if the aircraft is approaching an accelerated stall. Yes…it takes a little more work to hold that back-pressure during those turns. But what are the benefits of doing it without trim? Easier to recover from an inadvertent stall.
