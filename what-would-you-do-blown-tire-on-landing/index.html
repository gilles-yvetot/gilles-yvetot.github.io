What Would You Do? – Blown Tire on Landing

So what would you do in this situation? You’re coming into land and without warning you blow out a tire. Quickly you realize you’ve got no control over the airplane and are cutting it fast for the grass. What would you do? Can you imagine if there were runway lights? Runway signs? Other aircraft? Buildings?

What would you do in this situation? Leave me a comment below after watching the video!

Text Transcript

Hey everybody, Jason Schappert here of MzeroA.com driving back from the airport after a long day of flying. Actually our day ended..this is a question I wanna post to you… the day ended with a flattire. The left wing tire blew on 12 Romeo today. A little bit ofsideloading and a little bit of a hard landing with a student,that’s what caused the tire to blow. But the thing is, we literally lost all steering capability.

Now I was wondering “Why is my student aggressively going towards the grass right now?” so I jumped on the controls, and realized this airplane’s out of control basically. We went right into the grass thankfully there were no runway signs and we missed the runway lights and everything. This hasn’t happened before, never has it really veered off like that but it blew the left main. So I got a question for you. What is the proper procedure for something like that?

My first instinct was, we tried to get control of the airplane but it was really difficult. I realized on our projected route we’re not gonna hit anything so it’s fine. But think about if you are heading towards a runway light or runway sign? I mean my initial reaction would be, “Okay, pull the mixture back to idle” but everything happened so fast and we landed at 67 miles per hour. Things are happening real quick. So, are you gonna be able to think of that? Pull the mixture back to idle? SO I wanna see if any of you guys have a similar experience probably that you’d like to share. You can do so at the comment box below and also, what would you do if you’re in that situation? You just landed, you blew a left main or even blew a nose gear and lost control of the airplane effectively. Not really your fault, I mean, you blew a tire.

So what would you do in that situation? So go ahead and leave me a comment below. I’d love to hear what you think and what you would do in that situation and you can assure that you can get a response from me if you’re leaving that comment or if you’re watching us on MzerA.com and leave a comment on there. I can’t guarantee a response on any other video site but if you’re in MzeroA.com looking at this post, you can guarantee a comment back from me. So, anyways, this is all I got for you and remember, a good pilot is always learning. Catch you guys later.

    No way! We both blew a tire this week! Mine was on takeoff.

    I noticed that when it let loose (POP!) that the aircraft smoothly wanted to go to the left (the direction of the tire that was blown).

    I would think that the least you can do is get off the runway and let the aircraft pretty much do what it wants in terms of where it wants to go, but there is a bit of control on how fast and aggressively it goes that direction.

  • Dave

    I'm only a student pilot at 51 yrs of age with about 24 hrs but I have been around airplanes and hangars for many years. Like many enthusiasts, life gets in the way of our pilot dreams. I have also been a truck driver since 1980 and have much experience with rubber meeting the road.
    Similar to a blowout in a car–power to idle, steer straight ahead (straight line as much as possible), use brakes as little and as gently as possible. In a small single-engine plane that may mean no brakes at all. AFTER you have come to a stop and everything is under control (no smoke, etc.) call the tower and tell them what happened. You will most likely want to have the plane towed rather than attempting to taxi it for at least two reasons: 1. You don't want to incur further damage to the aircraft, and 2. You probably won't be able to control it adequately anyway.

    I have personally never had a blowout but was on board a plane that landed with an extremely soft tire and the above worked for him. The plane will have a lot of drag on the side with the flat tire if it is a main gear tire that is flat. In my experience talking with mechanics, the most frequent cause of blowouts is pilots landing with their feet on the brakes. I know it sounds silly but it's true. After you land and apply the brakes, it takes a fair amount of pressure on the top of the rudder pedal to lock up a tire, but if the brake is already locked before touch-down the wheel never has a chance to turn and it will burn through the tire and tube in a heartbeat. I have been taught to land the plane with our heels on the floor so we have good rudder control but no access to the brake portion of the pedal. Most people don't have big enough feet to apply toe brakes with their heels on the floor and it only takes a second to slide your feet up to the top of the rudder pedals after you are rolling down the runway.

    On a nosewheel blowout you should be able to use differential breaking and keep the yoke pulled back in order to keep minimal pressure on the the nose gear as on a grass runway landing.

    Hope that helps
