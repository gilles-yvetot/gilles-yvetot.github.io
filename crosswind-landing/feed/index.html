My vote is to figure out as early as possible the right amount of aileron for drift and the right amount of rudder to keep the plane down the centerline. For me that is as you get established on final. I call it crosswind muscle memory. I feel low hour students and low hour private pilots should not have to do a lot of movement of aileron and rudder when getting close to the transition. There is too much happening too close to the ground for me. That way “the cherry on top of the sundae ” never moves, which means the passengers never realize there even is a crosswind and the pilot is cross controlling down final. I proved this twice at two different times with two different students. At 1000 using rwy 06 winds 320 @ 18 G20 in a C152, at 1200 with a different student same runway 310 @ 20 G24. Same technique, same C152, same upwind wheel landing, with no flaps and little faster airspeed 70 knots, same results. Piece of cake sorry piece of Key Lime Pie.

]]> By: Flyingfish http://www.m0a.com/crosswind-landing/comment-page-1/#comment-6446 Thu, 03 Nov 2011 22:35:00 +0000 http://www.m0a.com/?p=3512#comment-6446 I enjoyed your feature on X wind landings. In the Late summer of ’67 I was sent back to New Jersey  where I would finish out my summer employment and ultimately return to school in Connecticut. I had not completed my pilot training but was close to taking my check ride. It was a hard sell but I convinced the FBO at Spring Valley, New York, just North of the George Washington Bridge, to qualify me for solo flight there  and I could complete my solo and cross country time. Spring Valley’s single runway ran East to West and unlike your airport was littered with ops shacks, maintenance hangers and the like and more often than not the prevailing wind was from the South. In the shadow of the Catskill Mountains it was gusty too. I had learned as a sail boater that the wind is often blocked by other boats, islands, trees all that stuff. There was no one to teach me, but I learned by necessity that cross wind landings required a little more speed and control in a steady breeze, never mind gusty conditions. The buildings provided a no wind situation just before and during transition then the wind would savage my 150 during the critical phase just before touchdown. (Gusty conditions)The crab came naturally. Mother is the necessity of invention, but most interestingly I was able to dope out that NO flaps really helped with control. I was flying the airplane down to the numbers. Other then short field situations that technique stayed with me and became part of my flying. I have never been criticized for it and it works well. In fact my check ride a few weeks later included a crosswind landing at Freeway Airport, (W00) ,  just East of  DCA in Arlington, Va.
