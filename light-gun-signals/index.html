Light Gun Signals

by Jason Schappert | 27 comments

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

Radio failure is no fun! Trying to remember your light gun signals makes the situation even more difficult! Let’s just say I had a very interesting flight today that involved a loss of communications. Check out the video below and then be sure to leave me a comment. – Jason


What did you think of my pickle I was in? What would you do? Or have you ever had anything similar? Leave me a comment below! I want to hear from you!

Text Transcript

Hey everybody, Jason Schappert of MzeroA.com. To say I had an interesting day today would be an understatement. I was flying over with a student. We taxied out and the circuit breaker kept popping on the intercom. You know, I had to push it back in and it kept popping back out. It’s not good to just keep doing it over and over; there’s a serious problem going on in there.

So, I decided, “Hey, we’re not going flying without an intercom.” We have no way to hear each other, let alone other aircraft. So we decided to just go ahead and taxi back in. It’s a controlled airport, no big deal. I brought it down to my mechanic who said, “I can’t do anything for you. You’d have to go to the avionics shop.”

Well, it turns out that the avionics shop is at the Class D — we all know Class D, you have to talk to somebody — Class D airport just west of the field. So now, I kinda have an advantage as I knew I was gonna go in there with no radios. So I was able to give the tower a call. I have them on speed dials. I called the tower and said, “Hey, this is Jason. Just as you know, I need to come in there, I got no radios.” And they said, “Hey, no problem, just tell us what time” and they told me, I dunno, left downwind for 36, just be on time and look for light gun signals. I said, that’s pretty cool.
But then I got to thinking, what if this happened on the air? What if it happened to one of my students when they were soloing? Would they have known the light gun procedures? That’s why I want to take a moment to go over the light gun signal procedures with you guys, just so you stay up to date with them. I was really excited… I mean, I had light gun signals for practice but I never actually had to have one and, during the day, it was a little bit hard to see it. You know, being so bright out from the tower. I wish I could have taken you guys with me and filmed it, but that would have been way too stressful and it was already a stressful situation enough. But enough of me rambling about my story.

Number one, I wanna hear you guys having stories. Lost communications, alternator failures, anything, circuit breakers popping out like in my situation. I wanna hear your stories so, on MzeroA.com, leave me a comment below and before you do that, I wanna go over a quick recap with you guys over the light gun signals just so we’re all in the same page of things and we’re staying up to date with those.

[Image is shown]

So this image you guys are seeing here, ATC Light Gun Signals, is actually taken from my newest book Pass Your Private Pilot Checkride 2.0 which will be out in just a few weeks. So, you get a sneak peek in some of the graphics in there.

Anyway, if you receive light gun signals.. the color Steady Green: on the ground, Cleared for Takeoff. In Flight, Cleared to Land. Flashing Green, On the Ground, clear to taxi, in flight, return to land. Steady red, in the ground, it’s just stop. In flight, give way to other aircraft. Flashing red, taxi clear of runway, in flight, airport unsafe, do not land. Flashing white tells you return to your starting point and it only applies on the ground. Alternating red and green means the same thing, exercise extreme caution, both on the ground and in flight.

Do I expect you to memorize each and every single one of these? No. Even on your checkride. I’ve had students say, “You know, I know the main ones. I know steady green is cleared for takeoff and cleared to land. I know steady red is to stop on the ground and give way to other aircraft in flight.” They know the basic. They know the gist of it. Another thing to say is, “Hey, I got this stuff on my kneeboard.” Have it with you in the airplane so you can look it up.

That is the most important thing because in a pressure situation where, say, you weren’t lucky like me, I didn’t know it would happen really. If it happened in flight in a high pressure situation you’re trying to troubleshoot, the last thing you need to be doing is trying to think about, “Whoa, what are the light gun signals? What’s the tower trying to tell me?”

Have them down in front of you. At most, kneeboards, print them out on ’em so you can have them in there. I might have this image available so you can print out and keep them in your flight bag just so you have that. But it’s so, so important that you at least memorize the basic ones and have them on your kneeboard in your flight bag for other situations.

Another important thing: lost communications, what do we squawk? We squawk 7600, that is lost communications. So you got your squawk codes, you can expect light gun signals. A good thing to acknowledge your light gun signals, rock your wings. When I came in there, they gave me, say, green light on downwind, I rocked my wings to say, “Hey, I see you. I know what’s going on” and to let them know we were on the same page. So that’s really, really good to know. Other things you can do is, say, flash your landing light. There are a lot of stuff that you can do just so you acknowledge, especially on the ground, just so they can see your landing light.It’s really important that you are able to acknowledge it back as well and be able to see them.

I encourage you guys to practice your light gun signals. If the tower’s not too busy guys, they might just say, “Sure, we’ll give you light gun signals if you like.” Just for practice. During the day, it’s really hard to see them. At night, it’s easy but man, during the daytime, it was really hard to see those lights. So something to, maybe, practice.

Guys, I want to, like I said earlier, hear about your experiences. Have you ever had light gun signals for real scenario or for practice? What was the situation or other in-flight things you want to talk about. Leave me a comment below. What do you think of the video? I love talking to you guys. Leave me a comment below especially if you’re on MzeroA.com, you know you’ll get a reply back from me. I can’t promise anything on Youtube because I don’t check it, but I do check MzeroA.com all the time for comments. So, Tweet it, like it on Facebook, whatever you gotta do, leave me a comment.

Guys, thank you for being a blessing to myself and to my business. And, most importantly, hey, remember, a good pilot is always learning. Thanks to all of you guys, see ya.

    Jason,
     
    I just found your site and I am enjoying it very much.  I want to share my experience with light gun signals I had about 3 months ago.  I am a private pilot with about 265 Hrs. to date.  We rented a 1976 Cessna 172 out of N. Perry/Hollywood airport KHWO in Florida.  We took off from runway 27 L with flight following and proceeded east to the beach then north along the shore @ 500 ft. to attend a training seminar at Pompano Airpark KPMP.  Just south of Ft. Lauderdale International KFLL, the radio started to flicker as I attempted to contact KFLL tower advising our transition northbound.  I put 7600 in the transponder after losing my comms.  There a was a jet landing on 27 R at KFLL as we passed just beneath him ahead of his wake turbulance.  No sooner did our transponder go out.  There we were in a heavy traffic area, no radio and no transponder.  Talk about tense.  As we approached KPMP, I turned west, rocked my wings while passing the tower and tried to go further west to maybe try and fix the problem and/or get my portable transceiver up and working.  Wouldn’t you know it, Murphy’s Law was in play and the transceiver had dead batteries; even I though I put them in the night before.  The switch may had inadvertinently been turned on and it was on all night.  But anyway, we turned east again and entered the downwind for runway 28 thinking we just left off RWY 27, KFLL was landing RWY 27, so the wind must still be blowing out of the west.  WRONG!  As I approached without flaps (electrical), I rocked my wings again trying to get the attention of the KPMP tower to get light gun signals, but to no avail.   I landed safely with a 12 kt. tailwind at KPMP.  As I got out of the plane a little upset for not getting any signals, I called the tower.  He told me that he did not see me and that the wind had just shifted and they were using RWY 10 instead .  Luckily there wasn’t anybody coming the other way.  It turned out that the electrical system had failed prematurely not allowing any time to secure a radio call or to give a position via the transponder.  Was there anything I could have done differently that I could put in my bags of tricks if this should happen again?  Please fell free to use my experience to pass along to your students – not to t@facebook-100000260194841:disqus
