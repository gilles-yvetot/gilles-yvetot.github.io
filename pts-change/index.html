  • Jeff

    It amazes me that most private pilots I’ve spoken to have no idea of the difference between a forced descent/landing (engine out) and an emergency descent (fire, for instance).  In case of a fire, they’d probably burn up while trying to stay at best glide speed.  My first instructor went over the difference and we practiced both.  I think it is a very good thing that it is being added to the PTS.

    Interestingly, I’ve heard numerous instructors talk about the “best way” of accomplishing an emergency descent and there’s often disagreement about the best method.  Flaps up versus flaps down, max speed versus minimum speed, tight spiral versus straight ahead, and coordinated flight versus a slip.

    I’d think that a commercial level pilot would have a far higher likelihood of experiencing an accelerated stall than, say, a runaway trim stall.  Sure, a CFI needs to know how to avoid and, if necessary, “fix” just about anything stupid a student might do, but a commercial pilot can easily experience an accelerated stall doing many of the things that commercial pilots can tend to do (tight banks at speed with near gross weight, etc.).

    Anything to improve safety is a good thing in my book.  I’m often amazed at how LITTLE is really required by the PTS.

  • Mark C.

    Well, I’ll be interested to see how the PTS say the accelerated stall is to be performed. If they limit them to 20 degrees of bank, they’re not much different than a straight power on stall and they’re not very realistic, as most people who will inadvertently enter an accelerated stall will do so at higher bank angles. And most people who do get into an accelerated stall will do so at a high bank angle near the ground and will not have enough altitude to recover regardless of whether they’ve practiced or demonstrated accelerated stalls on their checkride. As for an emergency descent, there are a lot of different opinions on how to accomplish it and it varies with the airplane being flown. Given those variables and the fact that most practical test flights never exceed 2500′ AGL, at least around here, I doubt that most examiners will test it. They may ask “what would you do if”, and if they like the answer then may say, “OK, show me that slip to the left”, or whatever you described. Still, if it gets instructors and students talking about it, (mine did), that’s a good thing.

  • Larry M. Diamond

    I am all for the emergency descent maneuver for the private pilot certificate. It was a maneuver I learned for my commercial rating that was discontinued as a requirement. This is a great tool for the student pilot to learn and have in their back pocket ready for the whenever they may need it(engine fire, sick passenger, loss of an engine over a great landing spot). It also teaches great control of airspeed while getting down as quickly as possible. It teach it in a Cessna 152 as carb heat on, power to idle, once in the white arc full flaps and start a descending 45 degree turn to the left. This holds airspeed at about best glide and gets the plane down quickly, but in control. As for the accelerated stalls, I am not a big believer in making it a requirement for the commercial rating. An accelerated turning stall at 30 degrees maybe, but I think the trim stall when going around would be the most practical.

  • Tony

    As you well know, an airplane can stall at any attitude, power setting. or airspeed. For years, all we heard about was approach (power off) stalls, and departure (power on stalls. The student should learn from the very start of introduction to stalls, the cross control stall, elevator trim stall, and the accelerated stall.  So, I am for more stall requirements for the Private Pilot, and not just the Commercial Pilot. Some say that this training would be too much for the student to understand, but my belief is if you give 150 %, the student will retain 100%. If you give less, the student learns and understands less. The accelerated stall is a killer of many good pilots who never had them demonstrated to them, or for that matter, ever done them.  I have a simple rule of thumb about NOT STALLING at LOW ALTITUDES.  This rule is: If you are at 1500 ft altitude or less, simply take away the two zeros, and double the remaining number.  So, 15 doubled is 30, the MAXIMUM bank allowed at this altitude should be no more than 30 Degrees.  If you are at 1000 ft, you should not bank more than 20 degrees and so forth. This rule is meant to keep the airplane flying clean through the air at low altitudes where  quick changes in bank and attitude can cause the accelerated stall and cause an accident.
