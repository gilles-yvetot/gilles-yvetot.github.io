I pretty much do exactly as you do in the video with one exception: I leave the carb heat on when I throttle back to idle. This is because that is what the configuration will be when I am below 2000 RPM in the pattern and especially when I cut the throttle on flare. Up here in Maine carb heat is our best friend (particularly from autumn to spring). I would like to know whether the engine is going to cut out on me when I have pulled the throttle to idle during landing, especially if for some reason I need to apply power and/or do a go around. I figure it is better to have the engine die while solidly on the ground rather than during the landing.

]]> By: Rich http://www.m0a.com/what-i-watch-for-during-engine-runup/comment-page-1/#comment-8686 Tue, 04 Jun 2013 01:48:00 +0000 http://www.m0a.com/?p=4034#comment-8686 An instructor of mine had a good recommendation years ago that I always follow now. I turn the fuel selector switch to the lower of my tanks on initial starting. I then run on that tank while taxiing from the parking/tie down area to the run up area. Then when I begin my run up I switch to the other tank (fullest), finish the run up and take off with this tank. This insures you’ve run the engine from fuel in both tanks before take off. Good idea in case you have water or bad gas in a tank.

– When checking mags, do the right one (2 clicks over) first, then finish with the left for on click back to both. You’ll NEVER end up taking off on one mag accidentally (done that!)

– Checking carb heat, let it sit for maybe 10 seconds. It can take some time for ice to melt, a brief check may not be long enough. (at least here in soggy, cool Seattle!).
