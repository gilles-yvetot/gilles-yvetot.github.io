Calculating Density Altitude

by Jason Schappert | 29 comments

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+

Calculating Density Altitude is vital to flight. Its something I’ve got to experience first hand… Think cessna 150 in the desert at high noon. The airplane just doesn’t perform like it does on a cool morning at sea level. Knowing how your airplane is going to perform before you crank it up is crucial!

Check out this video and then download the chart below!

  • http://www.facebook.com/MyScien Michael Mayes

    I normally just rule of thumb it. Normally gets pretty close:
    Field elevation + (compare altimeter setting to 29.92, subtract 1000 feet for every inch above, add 1000 for below ) + 120 ft * each degree above standard.

    Works pretty well and if you are used to it, it is a lot less complicated than it looks. Only things that get me is I still have to think about which side of 29.92 is addition and which is subtraction as well as I keep forgetting that 120 ft number.

    I don’t ever think I have seen this method be more than 100 ft off, which is pretty good.

  • Anonymous

    Great Question John!

    Ok here’s the scoop, the FAA definition of Density Altitude is pressure altitude corrected for non-standard temperature…. Now that doesn’t help us too much…

    The MzeroA Definition (I like the sound of that)

    Is… It’s where the airplane “feels” like its at.

    If density altitude on the surface is 3,500ft your airplane will fell like and perform like it’s at 3,500 sitting on the ground which GREATLY effects takeoff and climb performance.

    Hope that makes sense.

    High density altitude = less performance = more dangerous to pilots.
