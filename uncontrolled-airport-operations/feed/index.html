1. “Example: Dunnellon Traffic ….. and end with Dunnellon Traffic.” Actually, the word “traffic” at the end is incorrect. The first time you say “Dunnellon Traffic”, you are calling the station(s). But at the end, you are stating the AIRPORT you are at. You should not say “traffic” there.

2. Giving the N numbers is superfluous, as others have stated. I don’t care what your N number is… I care that you’re high wing or low wing, turbo or piston, etc. So all I want to know is Cessna turning downwind or Cherokee turning final or Stearman taking off, etc. If it’s all Cherokees in the pattern, maybe you could say “Red Cherokee”. That’s 4 syllables versus 8 for “Cessna Five One Two Romeo” and a 50% time savings in a crowded pattern.

3. My airport shares 122.9 with about 4 other airports all within earshot of each other. Brevity is extremely important on a weekend with nice weather. If you can eliminate the words “turning” or “runway”, it is of great value when there are 5 planes in the pattern at each field.

So, “Dunnellon Traffic, Cessna Five One Two Romeo is turning base for runway three five Dunnellon traffic” can be condensed down into “Dunnellon Traffic, Cessna Base three five Dunnellon”. That takes you from 27 syllables down to 13, and 1/2 the time transmitting, thus 1/2 the opportunities to step on someone else in a traffic pattern 20 miles away trying to work their way into the pattern. And everybody knows exactly what you mean, without all the extra words.

Always think about who is listening, and whether they give a crap about what you are saying. Consider what you’re going to say, and then say it as concisely as possible, removing every word that is not absolutely essential. Telling me you’re turning crosswind is of little importance to me when I’m inbound for the downwind. I am watching you lifting off and climbing out as I approach the field. I’d rather know you’re turning downwind because we are at similar altitudes without a lot of visible movement relative to me, and save that other time slot for someone at another airport who is turning final while someone is doing practice approaches. JMHO at a busy, busy, busy airport.
