]]> By: Jack http://www.m0a.com/how-to-taxi-in-a-crosswind/comment-page-1/#comment-3153 Thu, 03 Jun 2010 06:58:17 +0000 http://www.m0a.com/?p=2473#comment-3153 This is the same explanation of 'how to taxi in winds' that I've seen in a dozen other places. I think it's too 'high level' for the beginner student, especially since taxiing is probably the first thing a student learns about physically moving the airplane. The student is thinking: 'dive away? ailerons? what?' while still trying to apply pressure to the correct pedal and getting the right throttle setting and thinking about other planes on the taxiway and taking the right taxi routes and ATC and other pilots on the radio – it's a lot to take in at on time, and it seems like a lot of CFIs aren't thinking about how overwhelming it all is because it's old hat to them. And maybe they think that they're paying attention to the radio or to where they and other traffic are on the taxiways and don't think that the student is worrying about all that as well.

I think a better first-time explanation of taxiing in crosswinds would be more along the lines of:

If the wind is coming from [here], the yoke should look like [this].

Simple. Utilitarian. Makes sense to someone who is just starting to see what the airplane looks like from the cockpit, and what they physically do to control the plane. Then it would be easier for the student to sort it out later as to why, aerodynamically, that's how the control surfaces are held while taxiing in wind.
